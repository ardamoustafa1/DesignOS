# Persuasion — Ethical Conversion Psychology

These mechanisms work. That's exactly why they must be used honestly: dark-pattern
implementations convert this quarter and destroy trust permanently. DesignOS rule:
**every persuasion element must be true.** Fake urgency or invented social proof is an
instant review fail (`brain/quality-bar.md`).

## Social proof
The strongest B2B lever. Specificity is the active ingredient.
- **Hierarchy of strength:** named customer with face + result metric ("Cut onboarding 40% — Sara Kim, Head of CX, Loom") > recognizable logo > aggregate number ("12,400 teams") > star rating > anonymous quote ("Great product!" — worthless).
- **Placement:** one proof element per page section, rotating types (logos under hero → metric mid-page → deep quote near pricing → density near the final CTA where doubt peaks).
- **Similarity beats prestige** for niche audiences: a testimonial from "a 5-person agency like you" outconverts a Fortune 500 logo for SMB targets. Segment proof to audience.
- Numbers: real, current, specific (12,483 > "10,000+" > "thousands"). Unverifiable claims get cut or sourced.

## Authority
- Credentials that transfer: SOC2/ISO badges (linked to real reports), "Built by ex-Stripe payments engineers", press logos (only with actual coverage), expert authorship on content.
- Design itself is an authority signal: craft quality *is* the proof of competence — a security company with contrast failures has already lost (`industries/cybersecurity.md`).

## Scarcity & urgency (handle with gloves)
- Legitimate: real seat limits, real cohort deadlines, real price-change dates, actual stock counts. State the *reason* ("Price increases Jan 1 — grandfathered forever if you join now").
- Forbidden: countdown timers that reset, "2 rooms left" fabrications, evergreen "sales".
- B2B rarely benefits from urgency theater — momentum ("2,400 signups this month") is the credible substitute.

## Loss aversion (losses loom ~2× gains)
- Frame retention/upgrade around what's *kept*: "Don't lose your 14-day streak", "Your 3 team dashboards remain active on Pro".
- Trial-end UX: show what they built during trial (their data, their configs) — abandoning becomes a felt loss.
- Free tier design: let users create real value first; the upgrade wall should protect their *existing* work, never hold it hostage (export always available — hostage-taking is a dark pattern).

## Anchoring
- Pricing: present the anchor first — annual price crossed to discounted, or Enterprise tier leftmost/visible so Pro feels reasonable (`patterns/pricing.md`).
- Feature framing: "Replaces $400/mo of tooling" anchors value before the $49 price appears.
- Anchors must be honest comparisons (real prior price, real competitor cost).

## Commitment & consistency
- Small yes before big yes: free tool → email → trial → demo. Each step's ask matches earned trust (don't demand a demo call from a first-time visitor — offer both sizes of next step).
- Progress ownership: onboarding checklists ("3 of 5 complete") exploit the completion drive honestly — as long as the steps are genuinely valuable.

## Reciprocity
- Give first, visibly: free tools, calculators, genuinely useful guides, generous free tier. The unlock ask comes *after* value delivery, not gating it (content teaser-gating reads as bait).

## Framing & defaults
- Defaults are the strongest "choice" — pre-select the recommended plan/settings honestly; **never** pre-check consent, add-ons, or newsletter boxes.
- Positive framing: "99.99% uptime" not "0.01% downtime"; "Keep all features" not "Lose access".
- Price framing: per-seat-per-month for B2B comparability; "$1.60/day" only for consumer impulse pricing — mismatched framing reads manipulative.

## Where doubt peaks (map interventions to anxiety points)
| Moment | Intervention |
|---|---|
| Pricing reveal | anchor + guarantee + FAQ adjacent |
| Email/payment form | privacy microcopy, security badges, "cancel anytime" |
| Final CTA | proof density + risk reversal ("Free 14 days, no card") |
| Post-signup | immediate value delivery (the promise kept fast) |

## Anti-patterns (instant fails)
Fake timers/stock · invented testimonials/counts · confirm-shaming ("No thanks, I hate saving money") · hidden costs revealed at checkout · pre-checked consent · roach-motel cancellation flows · guilt-tripping exit modals

## Checklist
- [ ] Every proof element real, specific, and current
- [ ] Proof type rotated per section; density peaks at decision points
- [ ] Urgency only with stated, true reasons
- [ ] Anchors honest; defaults respectful; framing positive
- [ ] Risk reversal present at the highest-commitment CTA
- [ ] Dark-pattern sweep clean
